8.3 - Electric Fields

8.3.1 - The Electric Field​

Gravitational Fields and Electrical Fields​

It might sound strange that we are discussing gravitational fields. After all, shouldn't we be talking about electric fields? Well, as you've seen in previous topics, the concepts from mechanics are not all that different from E&M.

When studying gravitational fields, we knew that the gravitational force Fg\mathbf{F}_{g}Fg​ acting on a particle of mass mmm could be defined as g=Fgm\mathbf{g} = \frac{\mathbf{F}_{g}}{m}g=mFg​​. Similarly, electric fields can act on a particle of charge. And just like how a gravitational field exists around an object, an electrical field exists in the region of space around a charged object which is called the source charge. When a secondary object, called the test charge enters the field, an electric force acts upon it. That may sound strange, but consider the following: think about the Earth and the Moon for a bit. The Earth has a gravitational field all around it. If we removed the Moon and all other objects, then Earth's gravitational field wouldn't exert a force on anything. Sure, the field is still present, but there's nothing to exert a force on. If we add the Moon back, you'll see that Earth's gravitational field acts on it. Similarly, a charged particle has an electric field and when another charged particle enters it, an electric force will act on the particle that entered.

Mathematically Defining the Electric Field​

Key Equation

An electric field is defined as E=Feq0\mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}_{e}}{q_{0}}E=q0​Fe​​, where: E\mathbf{E}E is the electric field vector, or the symbol representing the field Fe\mathbf{F}_{e}Fe​ is the electrical force, as seen in Coulomb’s law q0q_{0}q0​ is the positive test charge which we placed in the field (basically the electrical equivalent of the Moon being placed in Earth’s gravitational field)

This equation says that the electric field from the source charge at the location of the test charge is equivalent to the electric force on the test charge per unit of charge.

Warning

The equation above only works for particles, or an object of zero size. For objects that have a defined size, things can get significantly more complicated and will often involve calculus or more complex math.

It’s important to note that E\mathbf{E}E is the electric field of the source charge, not the test charge. Furthermore, the existence of the electric field is always part of the source charge and is not dependent on the test charge existing. Remember: Earth still has a gravitational field, even if we removed the Moon. The test charge is only there to help us detect the electric field, just as the Moon helps us detect Earth’s gravitational field.

Key Equation

We can also write the electric field equation as Fe=qE\mathbf{F}_{e} = q\mathbf{E}Fe​=qE

The equation above tells us the force on a charged particle placed in an electric field. Just like before, directionality still applies. So, if qqq is positive, then the force is in the same direction of the field. If qqq is negative, then the force and field are in opposite directions.

E\mathbf{E}E is a vector and is in newtons per coulomb, or N/C. The direction of E\mathbf{E}E is the direction of the force on the test charge. For example, if E\mathbf{E}E is in the positive direction, then the force on our test charge is also in the positive direction.

We can state that an electric field exists as a specific point/location if a test charge at that point/location experiences an electric force. If you know the magnitude and direction of the field at a point, you can calculate the force exerted on any particle at that same point using the aforementioned key equation.

It’s important to note that these equations only work if we assume that q0q_{0}q0​ (the test charge) is extremely small and doesn’t affect the charge distribution of the electric field. If you were to have a particle with a significant amount of charge (q0′≫q0q^{'}_{0} \gg q_{0}q0′​≫q0​), then the electric fields end up changing. In that case, we can’t use the equations above. This is because the ratio of the force to the test charge is different. In other words,

Fe′q0′≢Feq0\frac{F^{\prime}_{e}}{q^{\prime}_{0}} \not\equiv \frac{F_{e}}{q_{0}}q0′​Fe′​​≡q0​Fe​​

Now, the equations will not work for the new test case. So remember: q0q_{0}q0​ has to be extremely small.

Calculating the Electric Field due to a Group of Point Charges​

What do we do if we want to find out the electric field at a point PPP due to multiple point charges? Well, we know that E\mathbf{E}E is a vector. Therefore, we take the vector sum of all the electric field vectors at PPP to find the net electric field at that point. This is just like how we add up force vectors to find a net force on an object!

Key Equation

The electric field at point PPP due to a group of source charges is the following vector sum:

8.3.2 - Electric Fields of Continuous Charge Distributions​

Let’s say that we have a group of charges and we want to calculate the electric field at a certain location or point. We already know that we can take the vector sum of all the electric fields, as demonstrated previously. However, it is often that these groups of electric charges are so close together that they can be modeled as continuous. In order words, these charges that are closely spaced and packed together are the same as a total charge which is continuously distributed along a line, surface, or throughout a volume. You can think of a continuous charge distribution like a bundle of charges all wrapped up together.

When we want to find out the electric field created from this “bundle,” we will have to divide the bundle into small parts that have a small charge Δq\Delta qΔq. Next, we use the equation for calculating the electric field for one source charge (see 8.3.1) to calculate the electric field at a point PPP:

ΔE=keΔqr2r^\Delta \mathbf{E} = k_{e}\frac{\Delta q}{r^{2}}\mathbf{\hat{r}}ΔE=ke​r2Δq​r^

Then, we find the total electric field at the point PPP by summing everything up:

You can think of this like finding the center of mass for a uniformly distributed element. For example, finding the center of mass of a plank of wood which has a uniform mass distribution. In fact, just like how you used the density equations for center of mass, you can also use it for charge. This concept is called charge density.

  • A charge of QQQ that is uniformly distributed throughout a volume VVV is called a volume charge density (ρ\rhoρ).
    • ρ=QV\rho = \frac{Q}{V}ρ=VQ​
    • ρ\rhoρ is coulombs per cubic meter (C/m3C/m^{3}C/m3)
  • A charge of QQQ that is uniformly distributed on a surface of area AAA is called a surface charge density(σ\sigmaσ).
    • σ=QA\sigma = \frac{Q}{A}σ=AQ​
    • σ\sigmaσ is coulombs per square meter (C/m2C/m^{2}C/m2)
  • A charge of QQQ that is uniformly distributed along a line of length lll is called a linear charge density (λ\lambdaλ).
    • λ=Ql\lambda = \frac{Q}{l}λ=lQ​
    • λ\lambdaλ is coulombs per meter (C/mC/mC/m)

And just like how you can have something uniformly distributed, you can also have the charge be nonuniformlydistributed. This nonuniform distribution can be over a volume, surface, or line. The amount of charge dqdqdq in a small volume/surface/line are the following:

  • Volume: dq=ρdVdq = \rho dVdq=ρdV
  • Surface: dq=σdVdq = \sigma dVdq=σdV
  • Line: dq=λdVdq = \lambda dVdq=λdV
